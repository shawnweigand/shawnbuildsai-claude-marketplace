# Comparison Framework

The comparison section at the end of the digest synthesizes findings across all competitors into three lenses. This is the most actionable part of the report — it tells the user what they should *do* differently or pay attention to.

## Section 1: Positioning Gaps

**Definition**: Ways competitors are framing their value proposition or narrative that the user's business is not.

**What to look for:**
- Messaging themes that appear across multiple competitors (e.g., all emphasizing "AI-native")
- Specific language, phrases, or claims that resonate with buyers
- Target customer segments being explicitly addressed
- Problems being named and claimed
- Tone differences (e.g., competitors going more enterprise-focused or more casual/approachable)

**Writing guidance:**
- Each bullet should name the positioning pattern and why it matters.
- Format: "[What competitors are doing] — [why this may matter to you]"
- Example: "Two of three competitors are now leading with 'compliance-ready' messaging — if your buyers are in regulated industries, this framing gap may affect perception."

## Section 2: Feature Gaps

**Definition**: Product capabilities, integrations, or offerings that competitors have publicly announced or promoted that the user's product may not have (or may have but isn't promoting).

**What to look for:**
- New features mentioned in changelog posts or announcements
- Integrations with popular tools being promoted
- Pricing tiers or bundles that address market segments differently
- Free trials, freemium tiers, or self-serve options
- Enterprise or compliance certifications being highlighted

**Writing guidance:**
- Be factual — only report what was explicitly announced. Do not infer.
- Format: "[Competitor] launched/updated [feature/offering] — this may be relevant if [context]."
- If you can't verify a feature exists, don't include it.
- Do not assume the user's product lacks this feature — frame as "may be worth reviewing" rather than "you're missing."

## Section 3: Strategic Direction

**Definition**: Patterns across competitors that suggest where the market is heading — not what any one company did, but what the collective activity signals.

**What to look for:**
- Multiple competitors publishing content on the same topic (signals a buyer trend)
- Multiple competitors announcing events in the same time window
- Pricing movements (all moving upmarket, all adding lower tiers)
- Partnerships or integrations all pointing to the same ecosystem
- Talent or hiring signals (if visible in public posts)

**Writing guidance:**
- This is the most interpretive section, but keep speculation minimal.
- Format: "Multiple competitors are [doing X] — this may indicate [market shift or buyer expectation]."
- Aim for 2–4 bullets. Don't force insights that aren't there.
- If no clear patterns emerge, write: "No clear directional patterns observed across competitors this period."

## Calibrating Depth

- For 2 competitors: 2–3 bullets per comparison section
- For 3–5 competitors: 3–4 bullets per section
- For 6+ competitors: Group by theme, 4–5 bullets per section

## Tone Reminder

The comparison section should feel like a trusted analyst briefing, not an alarm. Avoid language like "you're falling behind" or "urgent threat." Use neutral, informative framing that empowers the user to draw their own conclusions.
